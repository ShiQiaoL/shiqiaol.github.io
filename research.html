Research Directions

My work sits at the intersection of atomistic simulation, machine learning, and computational materials design.

I am particularly interested in methods that are both scientifically grounded and practically deployable, especially for heterogeneous energy materials and chemically complex interfaces.

01

Fine-tuning universal machine-learned interatomic potentials

I study how pre-trained atomistic foundation models can be adapted to specific materials systems without sacrificing transferability or simulation stability. This includes benchmarking training strategies, optimizer effects, and long-timescale molecular dynamics performance.

Representative methods: MACE, NequIP, CHGNet, PyTorch fine-tuning, validation across structures and trajectories.

02

Catalysis and reaction mechanism modeling

I use density functional theory and computational screening to investigate adsorption energetics, active-site behavior, and reaction pathways in electrocatalytic and photocatalytic materials. My goal is to connect atomic-scale descriptors with catalytic performance.

Representative topics: OER, ORR, hydrogen evolution, single-atom catalysts, heterostructured photocatalysts.

03

Battery interfaces and ionic transport

My research examines how local structure, chemistry, and defects shape transport and stability in battery materials. I am especially interested in solid-state interfaces, interface reconstruction, and the role of atomistic AI models in extending accessible simulation scales.

Representative systems: solid-state batteries, Li-S chemistry, heterointerfaces, defect-mediated transport.

04

Reproducible computational workflows

Beyond individual applications, I care about workflow design: dataset preparation, automated evaluation, HPC execution, and structured visualization. I value research pipelines that are transparent, reusable, and easy for others to build on.

Representative tools: Python automation, SLURM workflows, data curation, publication-ready scientific visualization.
